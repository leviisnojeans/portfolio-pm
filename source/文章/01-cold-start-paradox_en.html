The Cold Start Paradox of Consumer AI Products

Why most AI products are "try and leave" — and why some shouldn't try to keep users at all.

Weijie Liu · 2026 · Based on live streaming industry product experience and industry data analysis

Three AI products, three completely different trajectories. You might think: Sora isn't good enough, ChatGPT is good, Character.AI took the wrong path. This isn't entirely wrong — but the real issue is that we're measuring three fundamentally different product types with the same ruler.

The last decade of consumer internet taught us: retention is everything. DAU/MAU below 30% is dangerous. D30 below 10% is failure. This logic holds for ad-supported products. But for AI products, the assumptions begin to loosen.

Some AI products are transactional — users come to complete a task and leave. Midjourney doesn't need users to "stay", it needs them to think of it first when they need another image. Some are data-driven — every conversation improves the model. ChatGPT needs users to stay because without ongoing interaction, there's no data flywheel. Some are platform — users embed the AI into their workflow. Leaving isn't "uninstalling an app," it's "changing how I work."

This article won't tell you how to improve retention. It offers a diagnostic framework: first figure out which growth flywheel your product runs on, then choose the right cold start strategy.

2. The Growth Flywheel Framework: Does Your AI Product Even Need Retention?

Before asking "how do I retain users," ask: should I?

Original Framework · AI Product Growth Flywheel Typology

Data-driven Flywheel

Logic: more interaction → more data → better experience → more interaction. Examples: ChatGPT, Perplexity. Retention necessity: high. Cold start priority: interaction density — get users to talk, input, and give feedback. The imperfect product can improve, but only with data.

Transaction Flywheel

Logic: satisfy once → come back → satisfy again. Examples: Midjourney, Sora (should have been). Retention necessity: low. Cold start priority: intent capture — not keeping users in the product, but making sure they think of you the next time they need you.

Platform Flywheel

Logic: embed → switching cost → ecosystem lock. Examples: Notion AI, GitHub Copilot. Retention necessity: medium-high. Cold start priority: embedding depth — get users to put their assets (documents, code, workflows) inside your product first.

These categories overlap (Douyin is both data-driven and platform-like), but knowing which one your product most resembles matters more than perfect classification. Three diagnostic questions: Does user activity create a data feedback loop? Is there a reason to return after the core task? How high is the switching cost?

Classification is not the goal. The goal is knowing where to invest your cold-start resources.

3. The Sora Case: The Cost of Choosing the Wrong Flywheel

By buzz alone, Sora was hard to call a failure. 1M downloads in 5 days, quickly reaching tens of millions. But what happened after the hype? D7 retention of 2%, D30 at 1%, D60 near zero.

Sora's core job is straightforward — turn an idea into a video. This describes a transactional flywheel. But the product was presented more like a content community: browsing others' work, exploring trending templates, consuming rather than just "make and leave."

The Problem
On average, users needed to generate 10 videos to get 1 satisfactory result (source: a16z). The core value proposition hadn't stabilized before the product was positioned as a content platform. Neither path is wrong — but running both when you're sure of neither multiplies the downside.
The Lesson
For a transactional product, the greatest risk isn't slow iteration — it's telling a bigger retention story before the core result is good enough. When you choose the wrong flywheel, every step forward moves you further from where you need to be.

4. Relationship Stickiness: The AI Product That Did Half the Job

Live streaming, from a pure utility standpoint, is not particularly efficient. Users open a room, watch a streamer, scroll comments, occasionally send gifts. It doesn't solve a clear problem like search. But users stay, and they pay.

In live streaming product projects, two interaction-zone optimization efforts told a consistent story: danmaku decluttering (scroll rhythm adjustments → +15% ARPU) and tipping signal-chain restoration (+2.58% organic revenue). The common mechanism: when interaction signals become easier to perceive, paying becomes easier to do.

Original Framework · Three Layers of Relationship Stickiness

L1 · Social Presence

The user's sense that "someone is there." Source: Short, Williams & Christie (1976). In live streaming, it's the danmaku scroll and viewer count. Most AI products are weak here — users feel "a system is responding to me," not "someone is here."

L2 · Parasocial Relationship

The user's one-sided emotional connection with a virtual persona. Source: Horton & Wohl (1956). This is where Character.AI excels — characters have memory, tone, personality. Users return not because the tool is useful, but because they're "coming back to someone they know."

L3 · Co-presence

The perception of sharing a space with others simultaneously. Source: Goffman (1959). The energy of a packed livestream comes from this layer. AI products today are essentially blank here — they remain one-on-one tools rather than shared spaces.

Character.AI got halfway: L2 is strong (29min sessions), but L3 and the monetization signal chain are missing. Live streaming's lesson is that relationship-driven monetization requires signal design. Tipping isn't an isolated transaction — it lives inside a relationship: user sends gift → streamer responds → animation is seen → bystanders perceive "someone is paying to express support." AI products lack this closed loop.

Relationship stickiness is not a universal answer, and it doesn't grow from adding a chat box. Its core is signal perceptibility.

5. The Cold Start Strategy Matrix

Different AI products face fundamentally different cold-start bottlenecks. Some lack interaction data, some lack single-task quality, some lack workflow embedding. The problem isn't resource scarcity — it's resource misallocation.

Cold Start Strategy Matrix

Data-driven → Invest in interaction density

  • — Prioritize high-quality user interactions; data feeds the flywheel
  • — Don't over-invest in monetization or relationship layers too early
  • — Your most valuable cold-start asset is quality interaction samples

Transactional → Invest in single-task quality + intent capture

  • — Make the core task reliable first; solve "was this worth it?"
  • — Don't force social features or retention mechanisms prematurely
  • — What you really need isn't stickiness, it's "outcome memory"

Platform → Invest in embedding depth

  • — Help users bring their assets and workflows inside your product
  • — Don't polish isolated features without designing migration paths
  • — Cold start for a platform is "win the first plot of land," not "win the first minute of attention"

Cold start is not about lifting every metric simultaneously. It's about understanding what your product fundamentally depends on to survive. Get the order wrong, and even good execution can't save you.

6. Implications for AI Product Managers

1. Don't default to "retention" as the answer. For the last decade of ad-supported internet, retention was the unquestioned north star. But AI business models are more diverse. Some products deliver value precisely at the moment the user leaves. Before setting a retention target, ask: is leaving part of the job done?

2. Diagnose the flywheel before choosing the strategy. Low engagement? Low retention? Low paying rate? The root causes are different for each product type. Data-driven products lack interaction; transactional products lack single-task quality; platforms lack embedding depth. Without classification, fixes scatter.

3. Relationship stickiness is about signal design, not chat features. Can users sense "someone is here"? Is there continuity in the relationship? Are interactions visible and responded to? A chat box without these signals is just an input field.

4. Choosing the wrong flywheel hurts more than moving slowly. When the direction is right, slow progress still accumulates. When the direction is wrong, every step takes you further from your goal. Early on, ask: is what we're building making the flywheel spin faster, or just heavier?

5. Respect a product's right to be "use and leave." Not every product needs to be a high-frequency companion. Letting users complete a task efficiently and leave gracefully is itself a product capability. The real question isn't "how do I keep users?" but "does this product even need users to stay?"

7. In Closing

Return to the three data points from the beginning.

Sora's curve says: buzz is not relationship. When the core function hasn't proven its value, no amount of launch hype retains users. ChatGPT's curve says: embedding into workflows outlasts engineered retention. When an AI product becomes part of someone's daily routine, retention is a natural outcome, not a design target. Character.AI's curve poses a harder question: relationships exist, but the monetization loop hasn't closed. Between stickiness and revenue lies a missing layer — signal design.

Three products, three fates. The difference isn't just which model is stronger or which UI is cleaner. It's whether the team understood, early enough, what kind of user relationship their product was meant to build.

If it needs continuous interaction, spin the data flywheel first. If it needs single-task delivery, make each result valuable enough. If it needs relationships and shared space, don't stop at features — build signals, connections, and perceptible presence.

A user leaves an AI product not always because it isn't good enough. A user stays not always because it's the strongest. More often than not, what determines a product's fate is whether the team recognized early enough: are we running a transaction, a workflow, or a relationship?
