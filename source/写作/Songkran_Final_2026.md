# SONGKRAN: Field Notes on Legitimacy, Joy, and Who Gets to Take Up Space

This is an essay about a water festival. It's also about who gets to take up space, whose joy requires justification, and how marginalized bodies learn to self-regulate their visibility long before anyone asks them to.

As a strategist trained to read consumer behavior, I've learned that the most revealing insights come from moments of friction—when what someone wants collides with what they feel permitted to have. This is my field note from Songkran 2026, where I stood paralyzed on a skywalk above a queer celebration in Bangkok, watching the gap between desire and permission play out in my own body.

What started as a personal vacation became an unexpected case study in something I'd been theorizing about for years: how public space itself functions as a form of cultural negotiation, where some identities must continuously earn the right to exist loudly that others inherit by default.

---

## The Skywalk: Between Watching and Belonging

It was my first Songkran Festival in Bangkok. When the big day came, I took the metro to Silom Station, which is known for its LGBT-inclusive vibe. I didn't join the parade at first though. A mixed sense of euphoria and numbness struck me the moment I stepped onto the skywalk.

The euphoria made sense. This was my first pride-adjacent parade, and something about it felt like a quiet welcome — everyone easy and smiling, no one watching to judge. For the first time, the part of me I'd learned to carry quietly felt like it was allowed to just exist. The numbness was harder to name. It was the feeling of standing in front of something you've wanted for a long time and suddenly not knowing how to want it. I didn't know how to react. More than that, I wasn't sure I was allowed to.

I wandered on the skywalk, watching the parade crowd below, bouncing with the music. On one side, a rainbow sea of fully soaked people. On the other, commuters in office clothes, glancing down with wide eyes. I stood somewhere in between, and it occurred to me: this is what it looks like when the past meets the future. The version of me who used to keep his head down, and the version who flew here alone and doesn't quite know what he's becoming yet. I felt touched. Relaxed. Excited. And before any of that had a chance to settle, I reached for my phone to send a video to my friends on WeChat.

They replied: *"Haven't been back to work?"*

It was such a typical response that I almost laughed. But what I felt underneath was something quieter and harder to name. Part of it was the familiar recognition that some of the people I grew up with and I will live the rest of our lives very differently. Not because anyone made better choices. Just because paths diverge, and sometimes a seven-word reply shows you how far yours has gone.

But there was something else too. My friends get down to earth. They embrace reality as it comes — the job, the city, the timeline everyone seems to be following. And I've always been more romantic than realistic. I don't mean that as a compliment to myself exactly, just as a fact: I've spent most of my life trying to construct a version of it that feels right to me, rather than accepting the version that was handed over. Standing on that skywalk, alone in Bangkok during a water festival on a weekday, I thought — maybe this isn't an illusion. Maybe this is just what I'm capable of, and what I'm committed to.

Most of the time I feel confident about that. Determined, even. But sometimes, quietly, I wonder about the cost. Not what I've lost so far — I haven't lost much. But what might come later. Whether the people I love will one day have to pay some price for me being this way. That's the part I don't have an answer to yet. I held all of it for a moment, then put my phone away.

## The Economics of Solo Joy

So why wasn't I going down?

I've taken solo trips everywhere. I've dived out of planes and into oceans. I've tried so many ways of living and playing — how could I possibly be the kind of person who stands frozen on a skywalk above a water festival? But I did feel afraid. The water guns looked genuinely dangerous. More dangerous than skydiving, somehow. Was I losing my mind? I was literally wearing waterproof glasses.

The real answer, as I stood there watching, was simpler and more embarrassing: I was afraid of being seen as a lonely person. Specifically, by the kind of gay men who seemed to move through the world with ease — gorgeous, popular, never without a crowd. I've bumped into this feeling before. Clubbing, traveling, any group activity I show up to alone, I feel it. I bury it and keep going. Because my real reason for being anywhere is usually to experience something new, not to be seen experiencing it. When I travel alone, every part of my brain sharpens. The crows hovering over Roppongi's streets. The shattered surface of Florence's walls. The rivulets deep inside Ubud forest. I remember things better when I'm alone. But social norms have a way of getting inside you anyway, whispering that your value is measured by how many people showed up with you.

---

## Internalized Gatekeeping, Two Ways

The internalized homophobia here is its own conversation, and I've started to split it into two separate issues.

The first is what it does to me from the inside. I wanted to join the community and was afraid of it at the same time. I did try — I ended up in a group of cute navy guys through a connection on Grindr. But the dynamic was awkward in that particular way where you can tell you're not really being let in, and afterward my first instinct was to blame my own lack of charm. That instinct is the thing I'm trying to unlearn. Looking back more honestly: joining a group of strangers alone is hard. That's a social strategy problem, not a verdict on who I am. Progress, maybe.

The second issue is what it looks like from the outside. Songkran in Thailand has somehow become a symbol of "gay debauchery" in Chinese social media discourse.¹ I came simply because it was my vacation and the flight was cheap. But I still felt a knot in my stomach posting anything about it on WeChat Moments.² The responses range from silence to "how many cocks did you suck." It's hard to tell jokes from sarcasm, but I know how I feel. When an outsider — a straight person, or even a reserved gay man who's never been to a parade — says something like that, it lands differently than when it comes from someone who was there.

---

*¹ In Chinese social media, Songkran has become shorthand for excessive queerness—a way to other both the festival and the identity, positioning any participation as inherently transgressive.*

## The Cost of Volume Control

I finally went down. Not because I'd resolved anything — just because I didn't want to miss it.

The first splash of water hit me and I screamed.

A scream of pure joy. And then, almost immediately, a flash of shame.

I know where that shame comes from. When I was in primary school, a classmate told me my smile looked ugly. I don't know if they even remember saying it. I've been carrying it for decades. All that time, I've been managing how much joy I let show — in clubs, in crowds, around anyone who might be watching. Keeping the volume down. Trying not to take up too much space with my happiness.

But here's what it cost me: the exact things I want most. Being interesting. Being warm. Being someone people actually want to be around.

When that water hit me, something cracked open just a little. I was completely soaked, moving with a crowd of strangers, in a country that wasn't mine, celebrating a new year I didn't grow up with. And for a moment, I was just a person who was happy.

I'm making progress. Slowly, but I am.

## What This Means for Brands (and Anyone Building for Contested Spaces)

The skywalk moment—standing between observation and participation, between safety and authenticity—is not unique to queer identity. It's the experience of anyone navigating liminal spaces: immigrants, career-changers, newly diagnosed patients entering support groups, first-time buyers in luxury retail, anyone whose presence in a space feels like it requires justification.

For brands entering contested cultural territories—Pride campaigns, social movements, identity-based marketing—the lesson isn't about representation metrics or rainbow-washing. It's about understanding the *emotional labor* your "inclusive" positioning asks of the very consumers you claim to welcome.

When a brand says "everyone is welcome here," some consumers hear an invitation. Others hear a test: *Am I allowed to be myself here, or do I need to perform a version of myself that makes everyone else comfortable?*

The strategic question shifts from "Do we represent this community?" to "Do we understand what it costs them to show up?"

Because visibility—the thing brands traffic in—is not free. For some, it's inherited privilege. For others, it's calculated courage. The difference determines whether your positioning reads as genuine inclusion or just another space where marginalized consumers must earn the right to exist loudly.

The water guns at Songkran weren't dangerous. The gaze was. And that's the insight that changes how you build.
